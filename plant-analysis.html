Site Light & Heat Conditions

Measured Sun Exposure

Summer peak: ~5.5 hrs direct sun (midday window, ~10:30am–4pm)

Equinox: ~4.5 hrs direct sun

Sun arc: 147°–237° azimuth (SSE to WSW), centered on ~192° (nearly due south)

Blocked by: House walls block morning (before ~10:30am) and late afternoon (after ~4pm)

Classification: Borderline full sun / part sun. Most references define full sun as 6+ hrs. This site falls just short at 5.5 hrs peak.

Factors That Boost Effective Light

The raw hour count understates the actual light energy reaching these plants:

Midday intensity
All 5.5 hrs fall in the highest-intensity window (solar noon ± 3hrs). One hour of midday sun delivers ~2x the photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) of an early morning hour.
Elevation UV boost
At 5,900 ft, UV intensity is ~18% higher than sea level. Plants adapted to lower elevations may actually receive more effective radiation here than in 6 hrs of lowland sun.
Wall reflection
The SSE-facing green siding reflects diffuse light and heat back into the bed. Foundation concrete absorbs and re-radiates heat. This creates a measurably warmer, brighter microclimate.
Foundation heat island
Effective growing zone is 6a–6b despite being in Zone 5b. Warmer soil, earlier spring thaw, extended season. Plants experience conditions roughly one zone warmer than the surrounding yard.

Estimated effective sun equivalent: The 5.5 hrs of midday sun at elevation with wall reflection likely delivers PAR equivalent to 6.5–7.5 hrs of sun at a flat, sea-level, open-field site. This is solidly in the "full sun" range for practical purposes.

Future Shading Risk: Front Yard Tree

A small tree in the front yard may reduce sun reaching the bed now or in the future as it grows. The open arc (147°–237° azimuth) faces south, so a tree to the south or southwest of the bed would have the biggest impact. Monitor canopy spread annually — even partial canopy filtering during the midday window would push the site firmly into part-sun territory (4–5 hrs effective). If this happens, the part-sun alternatives listed below become the primary picks rather than fallbacks.

Current Selections — Sun Suitability

Each plant rated against the site's 5.5 hrs midday sun + microclimate boosters. Feedback from Tagawa Gardens nursery staff noted Amorpha and Agastache as the highest-risk species for reduced performance.

Dwarf Lead Plant

Amorpha nana

Role: Structural anchor, corner cluster

Sun need: Full sun (6+ hrs ideal)

Height: 1–2 ft

Special value: Nitrogen fixer, specialist bee host

At Risk — Tagawa's flagged

Analysis: Amorpha nana is a prairie species that truly thrives in full, open sun. At 5.5 hrs it will survive but may become leggy (stretching toward light) and produce fewer of its distinctive purple flower spikes. The nitrogen-fixing root nodules still function regardless of sun, so soil-building value is retained. Tagawa's staff specifically flagged this as needing 6+ hrs.

Its deep taproot makes it extremely drought-hardy. The hot foundation microclimate actually suits it well — it loves heat.

Part-Sun Native Alternatives

These replacements thrive in 4–6 hrs of sun but can also handle the intense midday heat and UV of the foundation microclimate. Only relevant if current selections underperform or if the front yard tree reduces sun further.

Replacements for Dwarf Lead Plant (structural anchor role)

Wild Bergamot

Monarda fistulosa

Sun range: 4–8 hrs — extremely flexible

Heat tolerance: Excellent — handles reflected heat well

Height: 2–4 ft

Pollinator value: Top-tier bee + hummingbird plant

CO Native Could replace Lead Plant OR Agastache

Why consider: Monarda fistulosa is arguably the single most versatile native perennial for this site. It thrives across a huge sun range, handles heat and drought, and provides outstanding pollinator value for bees, butterflies, and hummingbirds. The lavender flower heads are showy and long-blooming (July–September).

Tradeoffs: Spreads by rhizomes and can be aggressive in a 16 sq ft bed — would need annual containment/division. Loses the nitrogen-fixing benefit of Amorpha. Taller than Lead Plant (2–4 ft vs 1–2 ft), so placement matters.

Heat resilience: Native to hot, dry prairies and open woodland edges. Handles foundation heat island conditions without issue. Aromatic foliage may actually intensify in heat.

Front Range Penstemon

Penstemon virens

Sun range: Part sun to full sun

Heat tolerance: Excellent — native to hot rocky slopes

Height: 8–12 in

Pollinator value: Great hummingbird plant, native bees

CO Native Could replace Lead Plant

Why consider: A compact Front Range endemic that grows on hot, rocky outcrops — essentially the same microclimate as a foundation bed. Tubular blue-purple flowers are excellent hummingbird attractants. Very well-adapted to the exact conditions of this site.

Tradeoffs: Shorter (8–12") so less structural presence than Lead Plant. No nitrogen-fixing benefit. Shorter bloom period (May–June). May need replacement every 3–5 years as penstemons can be short-lived.

Heat resilience: Outstanding. This is a rock garden plant that actively prefers hot, well-drained conditions. Would love the DG strip zone.

Creeping Oregon Grape

Mahonia repens

Sun range: Part shade to full sun — widest tolerance

Heat tolerance: Very good — handles reflected heat

Height: 6–12 in

Pollinator value: Early bee flowers, berries for birds

CO Native Could replace Lead Plant

Why consider: Evergreen shrub that provides year-round structure — the only option here with winter visual interest. Yellow spring flowers appear early for bees, followed by blue berries for birds. Holly-like leaves are architectural. Extremely adaptable to sun/shade range.

Tradeoffs: Not a pollinator powerhouse compared to Amorpha or Monarda. No nitrogen-fixing benefit. Spreads slowly by underground stems — manageable in this bed size. Less showy flowers.

Heat resilience: Very good. Found naturally on hot, dry hillsides across the Front Range. The evergreen foliage handles winter sun scorch at elevation. Would be the most future-proof pick if the tree creates significantly more shade.

Replacements for Anise Hyssop (hummingbird magnet role)

Rocky Mountain Penstemon

Penstemon strictus

Sun range: Part sun to full sun

Heat tolerance: Excellent — very drought-tough

Height: 1.5–2.5 ft

Pollinator value: Excellent hummingbird magnet, native bees

CO Native Could replace Agastache

Why consider: The most commonly seen native Penstemon on the Front Range. Deep blue-purple tubular flowers are a hummingbird favorite. Very well-adapted to Colorado's heat, drought, and alkaline soils. Handles part sun much better than Agastache — it grows naturally on partly-shaded mountain slopes.

Tradeoffs: Bloom period is shorter (June–July, ~4–6 weeks vs Agastache's June–September). You'd lose about 2 months of hummingbird-attracting flowers. Not aromatic like Agastache. May need replacement every 4–6 years.

Heat resilience: Outstanding. Thrives in hot, dry, well-drained conditions. The DG strip and foundation heat would feel like home.

Note: Monarda fistulosa (Wild Bergamot) listed above could also replace Agastache — it fills the same hummingbird/bee magnet role with a similar bloom period (July–September) and much better shade tolerance. If only making one swap, Monarda replacing Agastache is the highest-impact change.

Recommendation

If Sun Stays at Current Levels (~5.5 hrs midday)

The microclimate factors (elevation UV, midday intensity, wall reflection) likely push effective light into the full-sun range. Keep the current plan as-is and monitor in Year 1. The two at-risk species (Amorpha, Agastache) may show slightly reduced flowering but should still perform their ecological roles. If either clearly struggles by midsummer, swap in Monarda fistulosa the following spring — it's available everywhere and establishes fast.

If Tree Shade Increases (drops to ~4–5 hrs)

This changes the calculus. Recommended swaps in priority order:

  1. Agastache → Monarda fistulosa — Highest impact swap. Same hummingbird/bee value, much better shade tolerance, similar bloom timing. Watch for spreading.
  2. Amorpha nana → Mahonia repens — Best structural replacement. Evergreen, extremely shade-adaptable, year-round interest. Loses nitrogen-fixing but gains winter presence.
  3. Keep everything else — Blanket Flower, Milkweed, Pussytoes, and Pasque Flower all handle 4–5 hrs adequately.

The "Try First, Swap Later" Approach

Native plants are resilient. A slightly underperforming Agastache still feeds more pollinators than no Agastache. Plant the current selections in spring 2026, observe through the first growing season, and make data-driven swap decisions in spring 2027 if needed. The alternatives listed here aren't going anywhere — they'll be available at Tagawa's or Harlequin's next year too.
