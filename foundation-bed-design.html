Design Concept

This narrow pocket between the stairs and porch corner calls for a layered texture garden rather than trying to cram in too many upright plants. The idea is to create visual richness through contrasting ground materials and just 2–3 carefully chosen plants that won't overwhelm the small footprint.

The design divides the strip into three texture zones running lengthwise: a planting pocket against the house wall, a band of decorative river rock through the middle, and Angelina Stonecrop ground cover creeping along the paver edge. One compact upright plant (Hyssop or Lavender) anchors the center, with a low Sedum mix filling around it.

The grey pavers stay — they frame the bed and match the concrete stairs. The existing small gravel gets replaced with a more intentional mix of materials.

Three Texture Zones

Zone A: Bark Mulch

Back strip along house wall. Dark cedar bark mulch contrasts with the green siding and provides a rich planting bed for the focal plant.

Zone B: River Rock

Middle band. Smooth 1–2" river rocks in grey/tan tones. Creates a clean, natural dry-creek feel and ties into the grey pavers.

Zone C: Living Edge

Front strip along pavers. Angelina Stonecrop spills over the paver edge, softening the hard line with chartreuse-gold foliage year-round.

Why this layout works for a narrow space: Instead of rows of upright plants that would look crowded and block the house, the three horizontal texture zones create the illusion of more depth. The single lavender provides height and fragrance without overwhelming. The living stonecrop edge softens the hard paver line and gives year-round color even when nothing is blooming.

Plant Selections

Scaled for a narrow ~1.5 × 4.5 ft bed. All are Colorado Front Range hardy (Zones 4–5), drought-tolerant, full-sun plants. Only 3 plant types keep it simple and not overcrowded.

Phenomenal Lavender

Lavandula x intermedia 'Phenomenal'
The single focal plant, centered against the house wall. Silvery foliage and fragrant purple blooms June–August. At 24–30" wide it fills the back zone without spilling into the walkway. One of the most cold-hardy lavenders — thrives on the Front Range with minimal water.
Qty: 1 24–30" tall & wide Fragrant Pollinator magnet

Angelina Stonecrop

Sedum rupestre 'Angelina'
The living edge along the front. Bright chartreuse needle-like foliage that turns coppery-orange in fall. Only 4–6" tall, spreads to fill gaps and cascade over the paver edge. Virtually indestructible — handles reflected heat off concrete and thrives in gravel.
Qty: 3 (4" pots) 4–6" tall, spreading Evergreen Year-round color

Creeping Phlox

Phlox subulata
A small accent patch near the stair end for a pop of spring color. Masses of pink, purple, or white flowers in April–May on a low evergreen mat. Softens the transition where the bed meets the stairs.
Qty: 1 (4" pot) 3–6" tall Spring bloomer Evergreen mat

Mixed Sedum (optional accents)

Sedum spp. — Dragon's Blood, Blue Spruce, etc.
1–2 small sedum varieties tucked into the river rock zone to add pops of contrasting color among the stones. Dragon's Blood gives deep red foliage; Blue Spruce adds blue-grey texture. These are tiny and won't crowd the space.
Qty: 2 (4" pots) 2–4" tall Optional accent Tuck in rocks

Installation Steps

Clear Everything Out

Remove all existing gravel, dead weeds, and debris down to bare soil. Pull out any weed roots. This is a small area so it should take about 20–30 minutes with a hand trowel and bucket.

Amend & Level the Soil

Loosen the top 6–8" of soil. Mix in compost and perlite — this bed is narrow and against the house foundation, so drainage matters. You want the soil level about 2" below the top of the pavers to leave room for mulch/rock layers.

Lay Weed Barrier

Cut landscape fabric to fit the bed, tucking it against the foundation. Cut X-shaped slits where the lavender will go (center-back) and where the stonecrop will go (front edge, spaced about 14" apart). Leave the phlox area near the stairs uncovered — phlox spreads better in direct soil contact.

Plant the Lavender (Back Center)

Through the fabric slit, dig a hole slightly larger than the root ball. Plant the Phenomenal Lavender about 4" out from the house wall so air can circulate behind it. The crown should sit just at soil level — lavender hates wet roots.

Plant Stonecrop & Phlox (Front Zone)

Space 3 Angelina Stonecrop plugs along the front, about 14" apart, through the fabric slits. Tuck the Creeping Phlox near the stair end directly into soil. Plant the 2 accent sedums into the middle zone where the river rock will go — just nestle them in.

Layer the Materials

Back zone: spread 1–2" of cedar bark mulch around the lavender. Middle zone: lay the smooth river rocks in a natural band, arranging them around the small sedum accents. Front zone: leave space for the stonecrop to spread — a light gravel or bare soil is fine here.

Water & Settle

Give everything a deep soak. Water 2–3 times per week for the first month to establish roots. After that, these plants are drought-tolerant — just occasional deep watering during hot, dry stretches. The whole installation should take about 2–3 hours.

Seasonal Care

Spring (March–April): Cut back dead lavender stems to new green growth — never into old wood. Shear the Creeping Phlox lightly after it finishes blooming to keep it tidy. Pull any weeds that snuck through.

Summer (May–August): Almost nothing. Deadhead spent lavender blooms if you want a second flush. The stonecrop and sedums are truly zero-maintenance through summer heat.

Fall (September–November): Enjoy the stonecrop turning coppery-orange and the sedum's fall coloring. Leave everything standing through winter — it provides structure and protects the crowns.

Watering: Once established (year 2+), this bed needs almost no supplemental water. The mulch and rock layers retain moisture while the plants are all adapted to Colorado's dry conditions.
