Zone 1: Downspouts & Drainage

Foundation Water Management

Priority: URGENT · Cost: $20–60 · Time: 1–2 hours

Why This Is Urgent

Multiple downspouts are dumping water directly at the foundation. Photo 1 shows active water splashing against exposed concrete with no splash block. At 5,900 ft elevation with freeze-thaw cycles through April, this causes foundation erosion, ice damage, and soil washout. Fix before spring rains intensify.

Action Plan

  • Audit all downspouts around the house — count how many lack splash blocks or extensions
  • Buy splash blocks ($5–8 each at Home Depot) or downspout extensions for every unmanaged outlet
  • For the patio downspout (Photo 1): remove vinca/myrtle from foundation base, install splash block directing water away from house onto pavers
  • For the side yard downspout (Photo 5): ensure corrugated extension reaches at least 4 ft from foundation
  • Grade soil away from foundation at all downspout locations — minimum 2% slope for first 6 ft
  • Verify the stairs-area downspout (Photo 2) isn't funneling water under the deck structure
  • Redirect toward beds, not just "away": at 5,900 ft in semi-arid CO, downspout water is a resource. Use flexible extensions to direct runoff toward tree ring, front beds, or lawn — 6+ ft from foundation. Free irrigation from every storm.

Shopping List

  • Splash blocks (concrete or plastic) × 3–4: $15–32
  • Downspout extension(s) if needed: $8–15 each
  • Optional: flexible downspout adapter for tight corners: $6

Zone 1b: Hose Bibs

Leaky Hose Connections

Priority: URGENT (needed for clover watering) · 2 bibs identified · Both leaking

Current State

Two hose bibs on the property, both with poor connections and leaking. This needs to be fixed before clover seeding — you'll need reliable light watering 1–2x daily for 2–3 weeks during germination.

Investigation & Fix

  • Locate and photograph both bibs — note which walls they're on
  • Check if the leak is at the bib itself (stem packing) or at the hose connection (threads)
  • Thread leak: replace the hose washer ($1) and/or use a new brass quick-connect adapter ($5–8)
  • Stem/packing leak: tighten packing nut behind handle (1/4 turn with pliers). If still leaking, replace packing washer ($2) or the whole frost-free sillcock ($15–30 + install)
  • Check if either bib has an anti-siphon / vacuum breaker — required by code for hose-end irrigation
  • Evaluate whether a third bib location would help (e.g., backyard near deck). Adding a bib is a plumber job ($150–300) — investigate but don't rush

Quick Fix Kit — Home Depot Run

  • Hose washers (multi-pack): $3
  • Brass quick-connect set (male + female): $8–12
  • Teflon tape: $2
  • Replacement frost-free sillcock (if needed, measure length first): $15–30

Zone 2: Native Lawn Transition

Buffalo Grass + Blue Grama — Clover Bridge Strategy

Priority: URGENT (timing-sensitive) · Cost: $95–150 · 2-year transition

Current State

Kentucky bluegrass lawn, dormant but greening up for spring. KBG is the biggest water consumer on the property — a non-native monoculture that needs 1–1.5" of irrigation per week all summer. The lawn is ~2,000 sq ft of the yard's total area, making it the single highest-impact zone for both water savings and native biodiversity.

The Vision: A Native Lawn That Runs on Rain

Goal: transition from KBG to Buffalo Grass (Bouteloua dactyloides) + Blue Grama (Bouteloua gracilis, Colorado's state grass) — both native to this exact location on the Front Range. Use Dutch white clover as a 1–2 year bridge to keep the lawn functional and green while buffalo grass establishes underneath. By 2028: a native lawn that needs zero supplemental irrigation.

Year 1: Spring 2026 — Clover Bridge + Buffalo Grass Seeding

  • April (soil 50°F+): Scalp KBG to 1.5–2", aggressive power rake or dethatch to expose soil
  • Overseed entire lawn with Dutch white clover (Trifolium repens) at 2–4 oz per 1,000 sq ft — mix with fine sand for even distribution
  • Lightly rake for seed-to-soil contact. Water lightly 1–2x daily for 2–3 weeks until clover establishes
  • Do NOT apply any herbicide — clover is a broadleaf
  • Late May / early June (soil 60°F+): Overseed Buffalo Grass + Blue Grama mix into the clover/KBG lawn
  • Water lightly for 3–4 weeks for buffalo grass germination, then stop all supplemental irrigation
  • No fertilizer. Mow at 3" max — favors buffalo grass over KBG

Year 1: Summer 2026 — Let Nature Select

  • Zero supplemental water after buffalo grass establishment period
  • KBG weakens and thins without irrigation or fertilizer
  • Clover stays green through dry spells — visual bridge while buffalo grass grows slowly underneath
  • Buffalo grass seedlings are small but establishing root systems
  • Leave clover flowers uncut when possible — pollinator food all summer

Year 1: Fall 2026 — Spot Seed

  • September: spot-seed any bare patches with more buffalo grass + blue grama
  • Do NOT overseed fine fescue — it would compete with the buffalo grass transition
  • Lawn goes dormant in October — that's fine and expected

Year 2: 2027 — Buffalo Grass Takes Over

  • Zero supplemental irrigation all year
  • KBG continues retreating — it can't survive on 15–17" of annual rainfall alone
  • Buffalo grass fills gaps via stolons, blue grama bunches fill in
  • Clover persists in moister microsites (near downspout redirects, shade pockets) — still providing pollinator food
  • Overseed thin spots in May/June if needed

Year 3: 2028 — Done

  • Buffalo grass + blue grama dominant, clover as minority pollinator patches
  • KBG mostly gone
  • Lawn runs on rainfall alone: ~15–17" annually in Highlands Ranch
  • Mow monthly, or not at all (buffalo grass tops out at 4–5")
  • Green June through September — exactly the months you're outside with the dogs

Why Buffalo Grass + Blue Grama for This Yard

  • Truly native: both species are indigenous to the Colorado Front Range — not imports like KBG or clover
  • Zero irrigation: evolved for exactly this elevation and rainfall (15–17"/yr)
  • Dog-friendly: durable under traffic, more urine-resistant than KBG, soft on paws
  • Ecological consistency: matches the native-species philosophy of the foundation bed and other plantings
  • Skipper butterfly host: buffalo grass is a larval host for several native skipper species
  • Blue Grama beauty: "eyelash" seed heads in late summer are iconic Great Plains grassland
  • Mow less: buffalo grass stays 4–5" unmowed, blue grama similar. Monthly or never.
  • No inputs ever: no fertilizer, no herbicide, no aeration, no dethatching after establishment
Water savings estimate

A native buffalo grass + blue grama lawn at this elevation needs zero supplemental irrigation once established. For a ~2,000 sq ft lawn currently on KBG, that eliminates roughly 35,000–45,000 gallons per season. The clover bridge year still saves 50–70% vs. pure KBG while the transition happens.

The Brown Lawn Question

Buffalo grass is warm-season: green June–September, brown October–April (~7 months). KBG is already brown ~5 months at this elevation. The difference is about 2 extra months of dormancy — and the green months are exactly when you're outside enjoying the yard. A dormant native lawn in winter looks intentional, not neglected. Colorado is trending this way — many Front Range water districts offer rebates for turf conversion to native grasses.

HOA Check

Colorado HB 21-1229 prohibits HOAs from banning xeriscaping, which includes native grass lawns. A buffalo grass lawn is mowable, maintained-looking, and green in summer. Still worth reading your CC&Rs to know the landscape — a mixed lawn (clover + native grass) during the transition year is indistinguishable from a conventional lawn.

Dog Considerations

  • Buffalo grass + clover are both non-toxic and soft on paws
  • Both are more urine-resistant than KBG — nitrogen-fixing clover handles urine nitrogen better, and buffalo grass is naturally tougher
  • Watch where the dogs run, dig, and lie down before planting beds — their paths are infrastructure, not obstacles
  • Foundation bed: Butterfly Milkweed (Asclepias tuberosa) is toxic to dogs if ingested — the bitter taste usually deters them, but note the risk for plant-chewers
  • Foundation bed bare soil patches (for ground-nesting bees) may attract dog digging — a few flat stones as subtle barriers help

Zone 4: Front Yard Tree Ring

Native Underplanting Opportunity

Priority: High (high visibility, easy win) · Cost: $30–60 · Time: 2–3 hours

Current State

Deciduous tree (likely crabapple or ornamental pear) with a brick paver circle at base. Inside the ring is bare — just grass and weeds. This is a high-visibility spot from the street, and the tree creates dappled shade in summer, making it perfect for shade-tolerant native underplanting.

Planting Ideas (Part-Shade Natives)

  • Wild Geranium (Geranium caespitosum) — pink blooms May–June, shade-tolerant CO native
  • Pussytoes (Antennaria rosea) — you're already using this in the foundation bed, great groundcover under trees
  • Creeping Oregon Grape (Mahonia repens) — evergreen, yellow spring blooms, bird berries
  • Wild Strawberry (Fragaria virginiana) — native groundcover, white flowers, spreads via runners
  • Mulch with wood chips (not piled against trunk — leave a 4" gap)

Keep It Simple

Pick 2–3 species max for this small ring. A mass planting of one groundcover (like Pussytoes or Wild Strawberry) with one accent (Wild Geranium) looks more intentional than a scattered mix.

2026 Seasonal Action Plan

March / Early April

Fix all downspouts — splash blocks, extensions, redirect toward beds where possible. Fix hose bibs (prerequisite for clover germination watering). Order clover + buffalo grass + blue grama seed. Check soil temp at 2" depth.

Mid-April (soil 50°F+)

Scalp KBG to 1.5–2", power rake to expose soil. Overseed Dutch white clover — mix with sand, broadcast, rake, water lightly 1–2x daily for 2–3 weeks. Continue foundation bed soil prep.

May

Foundation bed planting (Tagawa Gardens reservation May 4). Plant tree ring with native groundcover. Photograph tulips at deck edge. Stair tread repair when weather is warm and dry.

Late May / Early June (soil 60°F+)

Overseed Buffalo Grass + Blue Grama into clover/KBG lawn. Water lightly 3–4 weeks for germination. Then stop all supplemental lawn irrigation. This is the key moment — KBG begins its retreat.

June–August

Zero lawn irrigation. Clover stays green (visual bridge). Buffalo grass seedlings establishing. KBG thinning. Water foundation bed natives deeply 1x/week (first-year establishment). ID mystery plants (side yard grass, front shrub). Observe dog traffic patterns before committing to any bed plantings.

September

Spot-seed bare lawn patches with buffalo grass + blue grama. Expand front beds if foundation bed is thriving. Plant deck-edge companions after tulip foliage dies back.

October

Final deep water for beds only (lawn needs nothing). Mulch beds. Lawn goes dormant — expected and fine. Document what worked for Year 2.
